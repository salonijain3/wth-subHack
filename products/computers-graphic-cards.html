      About Graphics Card

      Upgrade your PC with Power

      A graphics card is meant to take the load off the computer's processor and memory systems when displaying hi-res video on the monitor. It is slotted into the motherboard and can be removed easily when its time for an upgrade or replacement. Used for HD games and video editing, graphics cards are excellent for people looking for an amazing gaming experience or editing high-end video.

      Great Graphics, Amazing Experiences

      The ultimate performance indicator of any graphics card is its model number, which represents a combination of graphics processor (GPU), clock rates, and memory bandwidth. The format is brand name + model number. When game performance is the goal, you should buy the highest tier graphics card you can afford.
      Most people think that memory size is critical when choosing a graphics card. That is the most common mistake consumers make. What one does need to pay attention is to the bandwidth. Always remember that GDDR5 memory provides twice the bandwidth of DDR3 at the same clock rate. When it comes to graphics cards, 1GB of GDDR5 is definitely preferable to 4GB of DDR3. Graphic cards range from 64-bit to 512-bit. These numbers represent how quickly information travels between the processor and the memory.

      Always Check for Compatibility

      Before buying a memory card, measure how much physical space your case has to offer. Be sure to check your computer's manual to find out the graphics card slot type before you purchase a new card. Most new models use the PCI Express slot. Also, keep in mind the output ports. Graphics cards come with a port where you can plug in your monitor, but if you also want to connect an HD television to your PC, look for a card with an HDMI port. Power supply is also a big consideration. Make sure your power supply has the wattage to run the card; high-end cards use a lot of power. Most high-end cards require 600Watts or more. While buying a graphics card, remember to focus on the bandwidth, memory and the number of bits, and you should be able to find exactly what you need.
